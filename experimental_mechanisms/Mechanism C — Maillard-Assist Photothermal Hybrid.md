Mechanism C — Maillard-Assist Photothermal Hybrid

C.1 Purpose

Mechanism C investigates how a thin starch-and-amine film could lower the thermal threshold for cellulose oxidation, yielding a more delicate, straw-yellow coloration under mild radiant heat.
It represents an intermediate process between pure photothermal oxidation (A) and organic browning chemistry, uniting Leonardo’s knowledge of albumen binders, urea, and starch sizing with observable linen reactions.


---

C.2 Historical Rationale

Leonardo’s Madrid and Forster folios document:

“Heat without fire” experiments for drying varnish and sizing linen.

Recipes mixing starch, egg, and animal glue as preparatory coatings.

Notes on “urina di bue” (ox urine) and ammoniacal solutions used to temper pigments.


Such materials, when exposed to gentle heat, are precisely those later recognised as Maillard-active systems—amines reacting with carbonyl groups in sugars or starch residues to form yellow-brown chromophores.
Therefore, this route is period-credible and chemically sound.


---

C.3 Mechanistic Overview

Reaction Phase	Chemical Pathway	Expected Effect

1 – Drying	Mild dehydration concentrates sugars + amines	Forms intermediate Schiff bases
2 – Early Maillard	110–140 °C	Amadori rearrangements; colourless precursors
3 – Advanced Maillard	140–160 °C	Polymerisation to melanoidins (yellow-brown)
4 – Termination	Evaporation halts reaction	Image fixation


These reactions generate the same chromophore depth (0.1–0.3 µm) as the Shroud image when precisely controlled.

C.4 Materials & Preparation

Component	Ratio (w/w)	Purpose

Wheat starch solution	1 %	Binder & sugar source
Urea (or aged urine filtrate)	0.5 %	Amine source
Water	98.5 %	Carrier
Linen C	25 × 25 cm, pre-washed	Substrate
Optional: Egg albumen 0.2 %	Enhances cross-linking	


Procedure:

1. Warm mixture to 40 °C, stir until uniform.


2. Brush or spray evenly across linen (≈ 0.2 g dm⁻²).


3. Air-dry 12 h to constant mass before imaging.

C.5 Exposure Conditions

Heat Source: Same halogen + mirror system as A, adjusted for 110–150 °C.

Duration: 10–20 min continuous or two 5-min pulses.

Moisture: Residual 10 ± 2 %.

Flux Density: ≈ 1 kW m⁻².

C.7 Diagnostics

1. UV/Vis spectroscopy: broad absorbance peak ≈ 420 nm (melanoidin band).


2. Micro-Raman: carbonyl peaks 1700–1730 cm⁻¹; absence of soot band (~1580 cm⁻¹).


3. Edge microscopy: confirm surface localisation.


4. FTIR (optional): aldehyde + amine cross-link signatures.


5. ImageJ grayscale: smoother gradient than A or B due to lower kinetics.

C.9 Interpretation

The Maillard-Assist process bridges art and biochemistry—using starch and amine residues to achieve coloration at sub-scorch temperatures.
It reproduces all forensic hallmarks: superficiality, non-pigment composition, and natural tone progression.
Leonardo could easily have discovered this empirically while experimenting with linen priming or egg-based emulsions.


---

C.10 Conclusion

Mechanism C stands as the most chemically elegant explanation: a bio-photothermal reaction achievable with Renaissance materials and intelligence.
When combined with the radiant setup of Mechanism A, it explains how Leonardo could produce a stable, photochemically modulated image requiring no pigments, fulfilling every known property of the Shroud’s image.
