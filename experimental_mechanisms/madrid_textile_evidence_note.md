# Madrid Codex I Textile Patterning Note: Evidentiary Status

## Technical Content and Workshop Capability

The cloth patterning procedure described in Madrid Codex I demonstrates Leonardo's practical knowledge of textile preparation, resist masking, transfer by dusting/pouncing, localized fluid application (including carved wooden stamp methods), and controlled wash-out sequences. The technical content is explicit: stretch cloth on a frame, apply glue resist to preserve outline definition, optionally use block stamps for repeat patterns, introduce acidic or coloring media to marked areas, and finally release the resist through soaking. Examples include multi-color applications (blue, dark violet-brown, yellow) and profile stamping. This is consistent with RWTH Aachen's treatment of the passage as a practical workshop technique, likely an improved variant of known cloth-decoration methods.

## Evidentiary Scope: Capability vs. Identity

As evidence for Leonardo's capability in textile-based image-making, this note is direct and substantial. It confirms he possessed exactly the skillset required for sophisticated cloth preparation, masking, transfer, and controlled surface treatment—all relevant to any theory involving deliberate image production on fabric.

However, it does not constitute proof that Leonardo produced the Turin Shroud. The described process is openly concerned with patterned, multi-color decoration (flowers, stamped profiles, resist zones), not monochrome body imaging. The Shroud evidence itself remains contested: STURP-affiliated analyses argue the image reflects superficial linen alteration without detectable pigment or dye, while McCrone's work identified paint pigments in a collagen tempera medium.

## Interpretive Boundary

The strongest historically defensible claim is therefore: **This folio establishes that Leonardo knew practical resist-and-application methods on cloth, which constitutes workshop capability evidence relevant to any deliberate textile imaging theory—but it is not, by itself, identity proof for Shroud authorship.**

The note belongs to the broader evidentiary framework: it demonstrates Leonardo possessed the technical foundations that would be prerequisite for sophisticated cloth-based imaging work, whether decorative, preparatory, or otherwise. It does not, standing alone, demonstrate that those foundations were applied to the specific case of the Turin Shroud.
